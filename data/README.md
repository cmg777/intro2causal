# Data Dictionary

This directory contains the datasets used in **Mastering 'Metrics: The Path from Cause to Effect** by Joshua D. Angrist and Jorn-Steffen Pischke (Princeton University Press, 2014). The book teaches five core econometric methods for causal inference — randomized trials, regression, instrumental variables, regression discontinuity, and differences-in-differences — each illustrated with real-world data from influential studies.

All datasets originate from the book's official replication archive:
<https://www.masteringmetrics.com/resources/>

## Chapter 1: Randomized Trials

**Key idea.** Random assignment of treatment creates comparable groups, eliminating selection bias. If treated and control groups are statistically identical *before* treatment, any difference *after* must be caused by the treatment.

### `ch1/NHIS2009_clean.dta` — National Health Interview Survey (2009)

**What is this?** A nationally representative survey of the U.S. civilian population, conducted annually by the CDC. This extract covers 2009 and contains demographic, economic, and health information for individuals.

**Why is it here?** Table 1.1 compares insured vs. uninsured Americans. The goal is *not* to estimate a causal effect but to show that insured people differ from uninsured people in many ways (they are older, more educated, wealthier, and healthier). These differences illustrate **selection bias** — the fundamental problem that motivates the need for experiments.

**Source:** National Center for Health Statistics, 2009 NHIS.

### `ch1/rand_initial_sample_2.dta` — RAND Health Insurance Experiment

**What is this?** Individual-level data from the RAND Health Insurance Experiment (HIE), the most influential randomized experiment in health economics. From 1974 to 1982, RAND randomly assigned families to insurance plans with different levels of cost-sharing — from free care (no out-of-pocket costs) to catastrophic-only coverage (high deductible).

**Why is it here?** Table 1.3 checks whether randomization worked — are people in different insurance plans similar at baseline? Table 1.4 (Panel B) estimates the causal effect of insurance generosity on health outcomes.

**Source:** Brook et al. (*NEJM*, 1983); Aron-Dine, Einav, and Finkelstein (*JEP*, 2013). Replication archive at [OpenICPSR #113919](https://www.openicpsr.org/openicpsr/project/113919/version/V1/view).

## Chapter 3: Instrumental Variables

**Key idea.** When subjects don't comply with their random assignment (e.g., a person assigned to "arrest" is actually just separated), we can use the *assignment* as an **instrument** for the *actual treatment*. This gives us the causal effect among "compliers" — people whose behavior is changed by the instrument.

### `ch3/mdve.dta` — Minneapolis Domestic Violence Experiment

**What is this?** Data from a landmark policing experiment conducted in Minneapolis in 1981–1982. When police responded to domestic violence calls, they were randomly assigned one of three actions: **arrest** the suspect, **advise/mediate** the dispute, or **separate** the parties (send the suspect away for 8 hours). The key problem: officers did not always follow their assignment. Some suspects assigned to "separate" were arrested anyway, and vice versa.

**Why is it here?** Table 3.3 shows the cross-tabulation of *assigned* treatment vs. *delivered* treatment, illustrating non-compliance. The random assignment is used as an instrument for actual treatment to estimate the causal effect of arrest on recidivism.

**Source:** Sherman and Berk (*American Sociological Review*, 1984); Angrist (*JASA*, 2006).

| `T_RANDOM` | int | Randomly assigned treatment: 1=Arrest, 2=Advise, 3=Separate |

| `MONTH` | int | Month of incident |
| `YEAR` | int | Year of incident |
| `V_RACE` | int | Victim's race |
| `S_RACE` | int | Suspect's race |

| `S_CHEM` | int | Suspect chemical influence |

**Key concept — Non-compliance.** Of 330 cases, officers complied with the random assignment about two-thirds of the time. The Stata code constructs dummy variables `z_coddled` (assigned to not be arrested) and `d_coddled` (actually not arrested) to estimate the IV "coddling" effect.

## Chapter 4: Regression Discontinuity

**Key idea.** When treatment is determined by whether a variable crosses a threshold (e.g., legal drinking at age 21), we can compare outcomes just above and just below the cutoff. If people can't precisely manipulate the variable, those barely above and barely below the threshold are essentially randomly assigned.

### `ch4/AEJfigs.dta` — Minimum Legal Drinking Age and Mortality

**What is this?** Age-cell-level mortality rates for Americans near the 21st birthday. Each observation represents one monthly age bin (e.g., "20 years and 3 months old"), with the mortality rate per 100,000 person-years for that age group.

**Why is it here?** Table 4.1 and Figures 4.2, 4.4, and 4.5 show a sharp RD design. At exactly age 21, Americans gain legal access to alcohol. The question: does this legal access cause more deaths? The data reveals a sudden, visible jump in death rates at the cutoff.

**Source:** Carpenter and Dobkin (*American Economic Journal: Applied Economics*, 2009; *Journal of Health Economics*, 2011).

| Variable | Type | Description |
|----------|------|-------------|
| `agecell` | float | Age in monthly bins (running variable). The cutoff is at 21. |
| `all` | float | All-cause mortality rate (deaths per 100,000) |
| `mva` | float | Motor vehicle accident mortality rate |
| `suicide` | float | Suicide mortality rate |
| `homicide` | float | Homicide mortality rate |
| `internal` | float | Internal-cause mortality rate (disease) |
| `external` | float | External-cause mortality rate (injuries) |
| `alcohol` | float | Alcohol-related mortality rate |
| `drugs` | float | Drug-related mortality rate |
| `externalother` | float | Other external-cause mortality rate |
| `*fitted` | float | Fitted values from polynomial regressions (for plotting) |

**Reading the data.** There are 50 observations — monthly age bins spanning roughly ages 19 to 23. The key comparison is between bins just below `agecell = 21` (cannot legally drink) and bins just above (can legally drink). The jump of approximately 9 additional deaths per 100,000 at the cutoff is the RD estimate.

## Chapter 5: Differences-in-Differences

**Key idea.** When a policy affects one group but not another, we can compare the change over time in the treated group to the change over time in the control group. The control group's trend serves as the **counterfactual** — what would have happened to the treated group absent the policy. This requires the **parallel trends assumption**: both groups would have followed the same trajectory without the intervention.

### `ch5/banks.csv` — Bank Failures in the Great Depression

**What is this?** Daily counts of the number of banks still operating in two regions of Mississippi during the Great Depression (July 1929 – July 1934). Mississippi was split between two Federal Reserve districts that adopted opposite policies during the banking crisis: the **6th District** (Atlanta Fed) provided emergency lending to struggling banks, while the **8th District** (St. Louis Fed) refused to intervene.

**Why is it here?** Figures 5.1–5.3 show a textbook DD analysis. Before the crisis, both districts had parallel trends in bank numbers. After the crisis hit, the 8th District (no intervention) lost far more banks than the 6th District (lending support). The difference in these differences is the causal effect of monetary policy.

**Source:** Richardson and Troost (*Journal of Political Economy*, 2009).

| Variable | Type | Description |
|----------|------|-------------|
| `date` | int | Date as numeric (days since epoch) |
| `weekday` | string | Day of the week |
| `day` | int | Day of month |
| `month` | int | Month (1–12) |
| `year` | int | Year (1929–1934) |
| `bib6` | int | Banks in business, 6th District (Atlanta Fed — easy money policy) |
| `bio6` | int | Banks in operation, 6th District |
| `bib8` | int | Banks in business, 8th District (St. Louis Fed — tight money policy) |
| `bio8` | int | Banks in operation, 8th District |

**Note on bib vs. bio.** "Banks in business" (`bib`) counts banks that have not permanently closed. "Banks in operation" (`bio`) also excludes banks on temporary suspension. The distinction matters because some banks temporarily suspended operations during panics but later reopened.

### `ch5/deaths.dta` — MLDA and Mortality (Differences-in-Differences)

**What is this?** State-by-year mortality data for young Americans (1970–1983), combined with information on each state's Minimum Legal Drinking Age (MLDA). During this period, many U.S. states changed their drinking age — some lowered it to 18 or 19, while others kept or raised it to 21 — creating a natural experiment.

**Why is it here?** Tables 5.2 and 5.3 use a regression DD design with state and year fixed effects. The "treatment" is having a lower MLDA. The outcome is death rates among 18–20 year-olds. This is the same research question as Chapter 4 (MLDA and mortality), but with a different identification strategy.

**Source:** Du Mouchel, Williams, and Zador (1987); Norberg, Bierut, and Grucza (2009).

## Chapter 6: The Wages of Schooling

**Key idea.** This capstone chapter applies multiple econometric strategies — OLS, IV with different instruments, and fuzzy RD — to a single question: *what is the causal return to an additional year of schooling?* Simple OLS estimates suffer from **ability bias** (smarter people get more education *and* earn more). The chapter shows how different IV strategies overcome this bias, and how each identifies the effect for a different subpopulation of "compliers."

### `ch6/AA_small.dta` — Child Labor Laws and Returns to Schooling

**What is this?** U.S. Census data for white men aged 40–49, drawn from the 1960–1980 Censuses. The dataset includes information on each person's state of birth, year of birth, education, and wages, along with indicators for the **compulsory schooling laws** and **child labor laws** that were in effect in each state at different times.

**Why is it here?** Table 6.3 uses child labor laws as instruments for years of schooling. States that required children to stay in school longer (before they could legally work) generated exogenous variation in education. The 2SLS estimates suggest a return to schooling of about 10–12%, exceeding the OLS estimate — consistent with the idea that compulsory schooling especially benefits those who would otherwise have dropped out early.

**Source:** Acemoglu and Angrist (*Journal of Political Economy*, 2000).

### `ch6/ak91.dta` — Quarter of Birth and Returns to Schooling

**What is this?** Data on men born between 1930 and 1939, drawn from the 1980 U.S. Census. This is the dataset behind one of the most influential papers in empirical economics: Angrist and Krueger (1991).

**Why is it here?** Tables 6.4–6.5 and Figures 6.1–6.2 use **quarter of birth** as an instrument for years of schooling. The logic: U.S. compulsory schooling laws require students to stay in school until a certain age (usually 16). Because school entry is determined by a birth-date cutoff, children born earlier in the year enter school at an older age and can therefore drop out with *less* schooling. Quarter of birth thus creates exogenous variation in education.

**Source:** Angrist and Krueger (*Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 1991).

| Variable | Type | Description |
|----------|------|-------------|
| `lnw` | float | Log weekly earnings — **primary outcome** |
| `s` | float | Years of completed schooling — **endogenous variable** |
| `yob` | int | Year of birth (1930–1939) |
| `qob` | int | Quarter of birth (1–4) — **instrumental variable** |
| `sob` | int | State of birth |

**Reading the figures.** Figure 6.1 plots average years of schooling by quarter and year of birth. A sawtooth pattern emerges: men born in Q1 (January–March) consistently have slightly less education than those born in Q4 (October–December), because Q1 births enter school older and can drop out sooner. Figure 6.2 shows the same sawtooth pattern in earnings.

### `ch6/clark_martorell_cellmeans.dta` — Sheepskin Effects (Texas Exit Exam)

**What is this?** Cell-level means from a Texas high school exit exam administered in the 1990s. Students had to pass this "last chance" exam to receive a high school diploma. Each observation is a score bin (e.g., "scored 2 points below passing"), with the average earnings and diploma receipt rate for all students in that bin.

**Why is it here?** Figures 6.3 and 6.4 use a **fuzzy RD** design. At the passing cutoff (score = 0), diploma receipt jumps sharply (Figure 6.3). If the diploma itself has labor market value beyond the human capital it represents — a **"sheepskin effect"** — we should also see a jump in earnings at the cutoff (Figure 6.4).

**Source:** Clark and Martorell (*Journal of Political Economy*, 2014).

| Variable | Type | Description |
|----------|------|-------------|
| `minscore` | float | Test score relative to the passing cutoff. **Running variable.** Negative = failed, 0+ = passed. |
| `receivehsd` | float | Fraction of students receiving a high school diploma (first-stage outcome) |
| `avgearnings` | float | Average annual earnings 7–11 years after the exam — **primary outcome** |
| `n` | int | Number of students in score bin (weight for diploma analysis) |
| `person_years` | int | Total person-years observed (weight for earnings analysis) |

**Reading the data.** There are 46 score bins. Look at what happens at `minscore = 0`: diploma receipt jumps from ~60% to ~90%, but earnings show little discontinuity — suggesting that the diploma credential itself has a small (if any) independent effect on earnings.

## File Formats

- **`.dta`** — Stata data format. Can be read in Python with `pandas.read_stata()`, in R with `haven::read_dta()`, or in Stata directly.
- **`.csv`** — Comma-separated values. Readable by any tool.
- **`.xlsx`** — Excel spreadsheet (output file, not source data).
